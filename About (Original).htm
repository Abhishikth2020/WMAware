About

The effects of plastic pollution has been known for years since 1907 with plastic being found in the intestines of animals such as dogs, buffalos and many marine animals such as turtles and whales. Plastic was also known to clog drains and release toxins in the soil. Being made from a polymeric material such as propylene (component of petroleum) compounds joined together to form a monomer (polypropylene), the energy to break these bonds is very high making it difficult for decomposition to take place. Moreover, the improper disposal of plastic such as being thrown out of a vehicle, thrown on the ground, and not being put in respective recycling bins has led to the increase in plastic pollution. Today, 420 million tons of plastic is being produced out of which 14 million tons are disposed of in water bodies yearly. Mostly found in Asian and African countries with inefficient waste management (garbage systems) systems, the worldwide effects of plastic pollution had even brought the attention of the United Nations to make a global treaty. Also, being made from fossil fuels, the effects of plastic were also innovative and revolutionary. During World War 2 (1939-1945), plastics were influential with the Society of the Plastics Industry forming. From February 1942, the plastic industry was beneficial for making war related tools. Also, plastics were used to make medical devices such as respirators and syringes with being a cheap material for making rocket chassis to enable cheap and efficient space travel. However, the rise of throwing single use plastics over time led to the abundance of plastic substances in ecosystems (taking more than 100 years to break down).



The implications of plastic being disposed without care impacted the world in negative ways from the accumulation of microplastics in the respiratory system leading to clogging and blockage of the alveoli and bloodstream (leading to shortness of breath). Also, the chances of cancer and cardiovascular disease from microscopic plastic particles inhaled is common. With only 9% of plastic recycled each year, the remaining particles of plastic are left to damage organisms and the environment by building up in water and air. Most surprisingly, when plastic is submerged in water, the small particles disperse leading to congestion in the digestive systems of ocean organisms. It is also concerning that a single styrofoam cup takes 50 years to completely decompose. When making this website, I was inspired to reduce the effects of plastic pollution in my community and to inform other residents, teenagers and older people about the importance of managing biodegradable and non-biodegradable (plastic) waste effectively. With this, the recyclability of plastics, paper, and steel would be preserved while biodegradable materials can be used to make manure (to improve plant growth). I also intended to inform readers and raise awareness about plastic pollution and the consequences of inefficient waste management. I have also gathered valuable information from direct interviews with residents who practice waste regulation and who are aware of environmental issues. Compiling research from many websites and articles to deliver reliable content also took place. In today’s world of globalization and sustainability, it is vital for eco-friendly practices to take place which would lead to increased popularity for conserving the enviornment and preserving resources.



Lastly, I want to mention about waste management. In high income countries, management methods are more effective and safe compared to low income (developing countries). Different companies such as WM (from the US) also make profits using waste management. Being a combination of collection, transportation, and disposing waste, waste management safely and efficiently disposes or recycles waste material. Today, methods such as using landfills, combustion, recycling, plasma gasification, composting, minimization (most viable and common for residents), and energy conversion are used to manage waste for productive purposes. Also, the importance of mass waste management is understood today with numerous startups and movements in India. It is impressive how environmental innovation is a major trend today which teaches about sustainability and productivity (for wastes to be useful). In other words, this is a way of creating something from nothing and I believe that this practice is beneficial for the environment and the advancement of adaptation and ingenuity.



Why was this website created?

While shifting through my daily life and when looking out of the windows on the bus, I was not surprised to find armies plastic covers, bags, bottles, and wrappers lying on dirt grounds. I have seen this frequently; but, I also felt curious to know why. When travelling to restaurants and shops, the amount of plastic lying on the roadsides and in the bushes of abandoned purpose was maximum. Also, when recently coming back from the US, I noticed that different bins for recycling (blue bin) and bio waste (green bin) were not seen often nor was there waste segregation. Instead, many residents (from other apartment complexes and communities) dispose all of their waste in one bin while pickup trucks do not segregate waste (or people wrap all of their waste in a bag and dump it in the truck).

While pondering about this, I have decided to use practices in my community as examples of how waste can be managed and regulated. I have gathered information from residents and committee representatives about their perspective on plastic pollution and waste management. Efficient waste management systems in households and residences can not only reduce plastic, but it can also effectively dispose of other wastes in respective categories. When waste is disposed through segregation, bio and non-biodegradable materials can be separated, biodegradable materials can be burnt for methane (energy production) or made into compost for plants, and non-biodegradable waste can be recycled, treated (toxic chemicals present) and reused. In simple terms, each category of waste is utilized to its full potential. Whereas, if plastic (non-bio) and soil (bio) waste are mixed, the hazardous dioxins and bisphenols would mix into the soil making it less fertile and more dangerous for plant growth.

When interviewing committee representatives, I have gained a wider range of knowledge based on sole experiences and practical knowledge from the subjects. I also knew that a website would be a very viable solution with respect to costs and efforts, minimum skills required, and the possibilities of organized planning to meet developmental goals. Also, I have chosen a website since multimedia can be used (such as photos, animations and videos). Additionally, I could modify the aesthetics in a website to improve the visual appeal. Lastly, the website would be easier to maintain while it can be shared to multiple people as a link rather than a large video file or application.
